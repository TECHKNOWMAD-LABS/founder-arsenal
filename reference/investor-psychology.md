# Investor Psychology & Behavioral Economics Reference

## Cognitive Biases That Drive VC Decisions

### 1. Herd Mentality
- **Effect**: When one prominent VC commits, others follow
- **Leverage**: Secure one strong lead investor; others cascade
- **Data**: Social proof effect is stronger when initial investors are well-regarded

### 2. FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
- **Effect**: Pressured to invest for fear of being left behind
- **Leverage**: Create genuine urgency with timeline, competing interest, closing deadlines
- **Caution**: Manufactured FOMO backfires; real demand is the only sustainable signal

### 3. Anchoring
- **Effect**: Initial valuation offer forms the focal point for negotiation
- **Leverage**: Set your valuation expectation first, backed by comparable data
- **Counter**: If investor anchors low, reframe with market data immediately

### 4. Confirmation Bias
- **Effect**: Investors seek info confirming existing beliefs; overlook warnings
- **Leverage**: Understand what the investor already believes about your space, then reinforce
- **Risk**: Can mask real problems -- use DD to catch what bias-driven investors miss

### 5. Overconfidence
- **Effect**: VCs are overconfident in both success AND failure predictions
- **Leverage**: A "no" often means "not yet" -- come back with progress
- **Data**: NFX research shows disconnects between founder and VC psychology

### 6. Pattern Matching
- **Effect**: Unconsciously favor founders/models resembling past successes
- **Leverage**: Frame your story in familiar winning patterns (if honest)
- **Risk**: Can lead to homogeneous portfolios; outsider founders face headwinds

### 7. Loss Aversion
- **Effect**: Pain of losing > pleasure of winning (2x magnitude per Kahneman)
- **Leverage**: Frame the cost of NOT investing (competitor will win, market timing, growth trajectory)
- **Application**: Show what happens to the market if they miss this round

### 8. Ego/Status
- **Effect**: Wanting to win "hot" deals leads to inflated check sizes
- **Leverage**: Create perception of deal heat without overpromising
- **Risk**: Ego-driven capital comes with ego-driven board behavior

## VC Decision-Making Framework

### What VCs Actually Evaluate (Research Data)

| Factor | Importance (% cite as important) | % cite as #1 factor |
|--------|----------------------------------|---------------------|
| Management Team | 95% | 47% |
| Business Model | ~80% | ~20% |
| Product/Technology | ~75% | ~15% |
| Market Size | ~70% | ~10% |
| Financials | ~60% | ~8% |

### Decision Timeline Inside a VC Firm

1. **Partner meeting pitch** (30-60 min) -- your champion presents
2. **Internal discussion** (1-2 weeks) -- partners debate; objections surface
3. **Deep dive / follow-up** (1-2 weeks) -- additional data requests, customer calls
4. **Partner vote** (1 meeting) -- typically needs consensus or near-consensus
5. **Term sheet** (days after vote) -- if approved

### What Your VC Champion Needs From You

- **FAQ document**: Preemptive answers to common objections
- **Customer references**: 3-5 ready to take calls within 48 hours
- **Competitive analysis**: Why you win, with evidence
- **Risk acknowledgment**: Proactive transparency builds trust
- **Follow-up materials**: Sent within 24 hours of any meeting

## Persuasion Principles Applied to Fundraising

### Cialdini's 6 Principles

| Principle | Application in Fundraising |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Reciprocity** | Share valuable insights/data before asking for investment |
| **Commitment** | Get small commitments first (advisory, intro, customer) |
| **Social Proof** | Name existing investors, notable customers, press |
| **Authority** | Demonstrate deep domain expertise; founder-market fit |
| **Liking** | Build genuine rapport; find common ground |
| **Scarcity** | Limited allocation, closing timeline, round filling |

### The Narrative Arc That Converts

1. **Hook** (10 sec): One sentence that creates curiosity
2. **Problem** (30 sec): Specific, painful, quantified
3. **Insight** (20 sec): What you know that others don't
4. **Solution** (30 sec): How you solve it (show, don't tell)
5. **Proof** (30 sec): Traction, customers, growth
6. **Vision** (20 sec): Where this goes if everything works
7. **Ask** (10 sec): Specific amount, specific use

## Timing Psychology

### Best Times to Fundraise
- January-March: New year, fresh fund allocations
- September-October: Post-summer, Q4 deployment pressure
- After major milestone: Product launch, big customer, strong quarter

### Worst Times to Fundraise
- Late July-August: European/US vacation season
- Late November-December: Holiday season, year-end portfolio focus
- After a miss: If you missed projections, fix metrics first

### Meeting Psychology
- Tuesday-Thursday meetings convert higher than Monday/Friday
- Morning meetings (before lunch) show higher energy from both sides
- 30-minute first meetings outperform 60-minute (forces conciseness)
- Follow up within 24 hours -- investor interest decays exponentially
